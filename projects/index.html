Bacterial hosts engage in antagonistic interactions with the lytic phages that decrease the survival of these hosts. To limit these negative interactions, bacteria must evolve ways to defend themselves and resist phage infection. This resistance can manifest itself in several forms: modifying the receptors phages need to attach, destroying invasive DNA or RNA once inside the host (restriction modification, CRISPR-Cas), or even early cell death to prevent larger outbreaks (abortive infection).

These mechanisms are only a small portion of the vast array of ways bacteria can defend themselves against phage. Why do so many mechanisms exist? One potential explanation is the other side of this picture: phage evolution. Phage mutations that can overcome the defense mechanisms of their bacterial hosts confer advantages to these new mutants. These new mutations can lead to new responses in the hosts to combat these new and improved phage, thus starting a cycle of coevolution.

This coevolution is the focus of my eco-evolutionary simulation model currently called CommunityImmunity. In this model, host and phage strains are allowed to mutate and create new strains that may be phage-resistant or may have a larger host range. The demographic information of all strains is tracked and partially determines the frequency of mutation. I am currently using this model to explore the impact of increasing host and phage mutation rate on the outcome of each simulation and the mode of coevolution.

Measuring microbial diversity through genomics is difficult due to the sheer number of individuals and species in a given environment, as well as genetic heterogeneity. To study the ecology of microbial communities, it is common practice to use marker genes, such as sections of the 16S rRNA gene, as a sort of proxy of actual diversity. I am broadly interested in the evolution of 16S and using it to measure global microbial diversity. I am also interested in how diversity at this marker locus corresponds to actual functional and species level diversity in bacteria.
